\section*{Headline findings}

\textbf{TL;DR.} The average per-word confidence inside a segment (mean of the model's max-softmax probabilities, one per output word) tells you the segment's overall quality with high reliability -- Pearson $r = +0.84$ against the Intelligibility Score. That single number is enough to filter the bad outputs out at runtime. The per-word green / yellow / red coloring on the kept segments only works \emph{inside} segments that are confident as a whole; in unconfident segments the coloring is misleading.

\subsection*{Term key (used throughout)}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{IS} -- Intelligibility Score (0--5), our 6-signal composite quality metric. Mean across the 1{,}497-segment baseline = 2.52.
  \item \textbf{NIV-Y} (``clearly intelligible'') -- a segment with IS $\ge 3.80$; the bar for a clean win, calibrated against an Opus-as-Judge gold standard.
  \item \textbf{NIV-N} (``not intelligible'') -- a segment with IS $< 2.00$; clear failure.
  \item \texttt{\textbf{mean\_word\_prob}} -- per-segment average of per-word probabilities; each per-word prob is the \emph{minimum} max-softmax across the sub-tokens that compose the word.
  \item \texttt{\textbf{mean\_prob}} -- per-segment average of per-token max-softmax probabilities (no word-level grouping).
  \item \textbf{WER / WWER} -- word error rate / weighted WER (high-value content tokens count $2\times$).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The story in six points}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

\item \textbf{Mean word probability is the single best confidence aggregate.} Pearson $r = +0.837$ with IS, $-0.714$ with WER, $-0.749$ with WWER ($n = 1{,}427$). Geometric mean, mean entropy, mean margin (top1 $-$ top2), and \texttt{frac\_p\_ge\_0.85} all sit within $\sim 0.03$ $r$ -- they are different shadows of the same signal. Spearman $\rho$ tracks Pearson $r$ within $0.06$ for confidence-vs-IS pairs, so the relationship is not a linearity artifact. \emph{Practical reading:} at runtime we don't need anything fancier than averaging the per-word probs the model already produces.

\item \textbf{Confidence is a strong segment-level filter.} AUC $= 0.917$ detecting NIV-N and $0.921$ detecting NIV-Y from \texttt{mean\_prob} alone -- both above the $0.80$ ``deployable as a single signal'' cutoff. At \texttt{mean\_word\_prob} $\ge 0.80$, we keep \textbf{78\%} of NIV-Y and admit only \textbf{9 NIV-N} out of 405 trusted segments (2.2\% false-trust). For zero-tolerance use cases, add \texttt{duration} $\ge 1.5$s AND \texttt{mean\_entropy} $\le 0.7$ $\to$ zero false-bads at $\sim 30\%$ recall.

\item \textbf{The green band's reliability depends on the segment.} Across 23{,}261 hyp words, a green word (per-word $p \ge 0.85$) is correct \textbf{80.6\%} of the time on average -- but that ranges from \textbf{18.2\%} (low-quality segments, mean prob $< 0.4$) to \textbf{92.8\%} (high-quality segments, mean prob $\ge 0.85$). ECE on raw max-softmax $= 17.4\%$. \emph{Practical reading:} show coloring only when the segment's \texttt{mean\_word\_prob} $\ge 0.82$. Below $0.65$, green reliability falls under 50\% and the coloring becomes net-misleading.

\item \textbf{Confidence catches hallucinations almost completely.} Of 223 hallucinated segments (WER $\ge 100\%$ AND length ratio $\ge 0.5$: fluent-but-wrong), only \textbf{3} (1.3\%) have \texttt{mean\_prob} $\ge 0.85$. Median \texttt{mean\_prob}: $0.541$ vs $0.759$ healthy. Median entropy: $2.82$ vs $1.20$. AUC for hallucination detection: \texttt{mean\_prob} $= 0.917$, entropy $= 0.913$ -- tied. The literature's worst-case ``fluent fabrications at $p > 0.95$'' is rare in this data; entropy is redundant.

\item \textbf{Different failure modes need different signals.} The 503 IS$<$2 segments classify (March 5-category taxonomy): Wrong Topic 68\% ($n=342$), Hallucination 10\% ($n=51$), Right Topic Wrong Details 15\% ($n=76$), Signal Loss 0.2\% ($n=1$), Accumulated Errors 7\% ($n=33$). Hallucination + Signal Loss ($\approx 10\%$ of bad) are catchable from confidence + length-ratio. The remaining $\sim 90\%$ require the reference -- Mission 6 (all-20-beams) and Mission 8 (topic-LM) are the next-sprint priorities.

\item \textbf{Trajectory shape predicts outcome.} k=5 k-means on length-normalized per-position prob trajectories yields a flat-high cluster (mean WER \textbf{31\%}, mean IS $3.89$, 64\% NIV-Y) and an uncertain-throughout cluster (mean WER \textbf{104\%}, mean IS $1.14$, \textbf{92\% NIV-N}). The shape difference is visible after just a few tokens -- viable as a runtime ``give up early'' hook (Mission 11).

\end{enumerate}

\section{Calibration: does the green band mean what it claims?}

The previous chapter showed that confidence \emph{predicts} quality on average. This chapter asks whether the per-word color promise (``green = trust without review, $\ge 85\%$ correct'') actually holds in practice -- and discovers that it does, but only inside segments that are confident as a whole.

\section{What confidence cannot tell us, and what's next}
% =====================================================================

Confidence has three blind spots, all visible in the failure-mode breakdown:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Wrong Topic} (68\% of bad segments). The model commits decisively to a wrong domain; \texttt{mean\_prob} sits in the medium band, no signal in confidence space sharp enough to flag. Needs semantic similarity (reference-required) or a runtime substitute -- a topic-conditioned LM scoring the hyp for surprise relative to the source video's topic. \emph{Mission 8.}
  \item \textbf{Right Topic, Wrong Details} (15\% of bad segments). Confidence is the closest of any failure mode to healthy. The model knows the topic but loses the specific entities (numbers, proper nouns). Needs NEA F1 (reference-required) or beam disagreement -- if the chosen beam dropped a number that other beams kept, that's a flag. \emph{Mission 6 (all-20-beams capture).}
  \item \textbf{The ``billion $\to$ million'' problem} -- a single high-confidence wrong content word inside an otherwise-fine segment. Bypasses all current signals because the model is decisive on the wrong answer. Needs source-context priors (was the conversation about money?) or visual disambiguation. \emph{Long-term.}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Recommendations.}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Keep CONF\_HIGH = 0.85 / CONF\_MED = 0.40 for per-word coloring}, but gate the \emph{display} on segment-level \texttt{mean\_word\_prob} $\ge 0.82$. Below that threshold, hide the segment or banner ``unreliable -- do not trust greens.'' Sections 2.2--2.4.
  \item \textbf{Use \texttt{mean\_word\_prob} $\ge 0.80$ as the runtime quality gate.} Already exposed via \texttt{make\_report.py --word-confidence} as \texttt{sentence\_confidence}. Section 3.2.
  \item \textbf{Don't promote entropy to a primary gate.} Tied with max-softmax (Section 4.1), harder to explain. Keep capturing it for archaeology.
  \item \textbf{Mission 6 (all-20-beams capture) is the highest-leverage next-sprint item.} Top-3 entropy is $r = 0.95$ redundant with full entropy (Section 1.4); we need genuine beam disagreement to expose Right-Topic-Wrong-Details and the dangerous decisive-but-wrong cases.
  \item \textbf{Mission 8 (topic-conditioned LM for hyp rescoring) handles the Wrong Topic hole.} 68\% of bad segments fall here; the reference contains topic context that a runtime LM could approximate.
  \item \textbf{Mission 11 (mid-decode trajectory monitoring) is the runtime ``give up early'' hook.} Section 4.3 shows the flat-low cluster is identifiable from just a few tokens.
\end{enumerate}
